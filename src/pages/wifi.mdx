# The Evolution of Wi-Fi

## What is Wi-Fi?

Wi-Fi is a wireless technology that allows devices like smartphones, laptops, and other devices to exchange data and reach the internet without a physical cable.

## Wireless Communication

Wireless communication is the process of sending and receiving data over a distance without using physical cables or wires. Instead of cords, it relies on invisible electromagnetic waves or radio signals to carry information through the air. Any type of data that can be turned into digital code—including phone calls, text messages, photos, and streaming videos—can be transmitted this way.

<TextWithImage
  heading="Why Wi-Fi Needs Collision Avoidance (CSMA/CA)"
  body={`Every Wi-Fi generation from 1997 through Wi-Fi 6 relies on **Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision Avoidance (CSMA/CA)** because all devices on a wireless network share a single Radio frequency channel. Before transmitting, a device must "listen" to ensure the channel is completely quiet.

Without this rule, devices would transmit simultaneously, causing their radio signals to interfere and cancel each other out. As shown in the diagram, this results in a collision that corrupts the data, forcing both Client 1 and Client 2 to reset, wait, and retry.

While this first-come, first-served system works well with light traffic, it creates a massive bottleneck as more devices are added. To solve this structural limitation, Wi-Fi 6 introduced OFDMA to allow parallel, multi-user communication without collisions.`}
